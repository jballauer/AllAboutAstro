This appendix is meant to eventually be a proper, dedicated study of K&E's case and box designs — the leathers, linings, hardware, and packaging options offered alongside their rules, and how those changed over the decades. That focused research hasn't happened yet. In the meantime, what follows is a running collection of what has already turned up about K&E cases elsewhere in this book, gathered here as a starting point rather than a finished survey.

**Leather grades and bundling.** For much of the 20th century, K&E's higher-end duplex rules were offered with a choice of case: a "synthetic leather" case at the base price, or a real-leather, chamois-lined case for a small upcharge. Some catalog numbers even distinguished the bundle explicitly — the Model 68-1220 Deci-Trig, for example, was cataloged separately as 68-1220 (synthetic leather case), 68-1215 (real leather, chamois-lined case), and 68-1210 (the nicer case bundled with a hardback manual), all for the same rule. See [Chapter 3](/sliderules/all-about-ke-rules/chapter-3-double-sided-duplex-type/) for pricing on these options across several duplex models.

**Slip-cases with a clip.** Not every case was a simple sleeve. The Model 4153, K&E's late-1950s pocket Polyphase Mannheim, came in two case configurations: a plain pocket sheath (4153-1) or a leather slip-case fitted with a leather-covered metal spring clip (4153C), the latter costing about 75 cents more in 1958. See [Chapter 2](/sliderules/all-about-ke-rules/chapter-2-single-sided-mannheim-type/).

**Box-jointed mahogany cases.** The larger cylindrical calculators K&E distributed — the Thacher, for one — came in their own box-jointed mahogany cases rather than a leather sleeve, befitting a desktop instrument rather than a pocket one. See [Chapter 5](/sliderules/all-about-ke-rules/chapter-5-miscellaneous-rules/).

**Government and institutional cases.** K&E's out-of-catalog government and military rules often shipped in cases distinct from their catalog counterparts — identification tags stamped directly onto government-issue leather cases, and the Belleville Spring Washer Computor's case bearing the Associated Spring Corporation's own branding rather than K&E's. The military's own technical manual for the Rule, Slide, Military, Field Artillery even specifies "With Case" as part of the item's formal name. See [Chapter 6](/sliderules/all-about-ke-rules/chapter-6-out-of-catalog-custom-rules/).

**Case as a dating tool.** Collectors can sometimes use case and cursor style together to narrow down a rule's production era when the serial number alone is ambiguous — useful in periods where K&E's serial-number sequence rolled over more than once. See the [portfolio](/sliderules/all-about-ke-rules/chapter-7-portfolio/) entries for examples of this in practice.

As the rest of the collection gets photographed and cataloged in more depth, expect this appendix to grow into an actual side-by-side study of case materials, hardware, and linings across the decades — rather than just a set of pointers back into the chapters.
